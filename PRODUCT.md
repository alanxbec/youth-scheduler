# Product

## Register

product

## Platform

web

## Users

Two audiences. Primary: foster youth (teens), opening a texted link on a phone — often older hardware, spotty data, no patience for logins or clutter. Their job: pick a meeting time in under a minute. Secondary: the case manager (CM), a busy nonprofit worker on a laptop, checking and managing their week each morning.

## Product Purpose

Weekly Check-In Scheduler lets each case manager share one link; their youth pick a weekday meeting slot identified only by initials. The CM sees and controls only their own Mon–Fri calendar. Success: youth book without help, CMs stop playing phone tag, and no youth's full name ever appears in the system.

## Positioning

The fastest, most private way for a foster youth to lock in their weekly check-in — one tap, initials, done.

## Brand Personality

Calm, trustworthy, quietly hopeful. Warm without being childish; institutional without being cold. The youth page should feel like a kind text message, not a government form.

## Anti-references

Not Calendly's dense multi-timezone grid. Not a hospital-portal gray form. Not a gamified teen app with confetti and mascots. No cream/beige "nonprofit warmth" wash — warmth lives in the green, the copy, and the generous touch targets.

## Design Principles

1. One-minute booking: every screen on the youth path has exactly one decision.
2. Initials only: privacy is a feature; the UI never asks for more than it needs.
3. Earned familiarity: standard controls, standard patterns — the tool disappears into the task.
4. Thumb-first: the youth surface is designed for one hand on a small phone, then adapted up.
5. One-person maintainable: fewer files, readable code, no cleverness.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG 2.1 AA. 4.5:1 body contrast, 44px+ touch targets on the youth page, full keyboard operability on the dashboard, visible focus rings, reduced-motion alternatives, semantic HTML (real buttons, labels, aria-live for async errors).
